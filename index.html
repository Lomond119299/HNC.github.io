Communication Skills



Communication Skills
Listening

When I started this course, I had a reality check when it came to listening. Relating to Egan's shadow side of listening, I was not engaging with clients because I was rehearsing what I was going to say rather than attempting to listen to them and empathise. Many of my responses were ineffective and tokenistic because I felt that I needed to do them to pass rather than because I felt that they were of therapeutic value.

I feel that I have majorly improved however, as the stress of ‘getting it right’ has subsided. Though, I am still afraid to take a moment to pause and think of what I am going to say after the client has stopped speaking - I still rehearse what I’m going to say.

My listening outwith counselling sessions have radically improved too. When I hear an opinion I disagree with, I try to put myself in their shoes and empathise with them rather than let my visceral reactions dominate my response. I have a greater appreciation for phenomenology. This, of course, helps me in the counselling room too - not all clients will say favourable things, but it is not my role to agree or disagree with these views and opinions. 		Speaking

In my first counselling session, I struggled to speak about myself for even ten minutes. I felt confused. I had this impression of myself that I couldn’t shut up, but when it came to exploring my emotions I think I still felt really guarded and defensive in being genuine.

Additionally, as the counsellor, I found that I was often uncomfortable in taking risks and getting it wrong. I struggled to empathise a lot and, while I think I have made improvements, I feel that I have a long way to go in working on this.

I find it a lot easier to open and talk about myself in a genuine manner now. I still use humour as a defence mechanism, but I am trying to move past that. I often feel that I can relate with Gestalt Therapy's idea of Egotism: I delete texts when people didn't respond out of fear that they had negatively judged me. I've made progress in stopping this, and this course has helped raise my self-awareness, but I’m aware of this external locus of evaluation still.
Vocabulary

I regard vocabulary as a distinct element in communication, especially in this course.

Initially, I reused the same basic emotion words – sad, mad, happy. I felt I was struggling to accurately reflect clients’ feelings. I have since improved my language surrounding feelings, especially since reading Therapeutic Communication by D. Corydon Hammond, Dean H. Hepworth, and Veon G. Smith. 		Non-Verbal and Body Communication

I think I struggle a lot in this outwith counselling sessions.

In the counselling sessions, I adopt SOLER and ensure that I come across in an open manner with clients. I also try to use a softer voice when speaking so as not to come across harsh - rather, I want to seem sincere and gentle in responding to clients.

However, outwith sessions, I feel that I struggle to come across the right way or use the right body language to convey what I'm trying to convey. This is an area that I'd like to work on more.

Theoretical approaches to counselling



My knowledge of theory, while enough to pass this course, could do with a lot more development and work, as I don't feel competent or confident in working with any of them. Beyond the Graded Unit, I never had to apply a theory to practice - and I think it would be dangerous to try and do so, given that I do not fully comprehend any of these theories. However, my understanding of the Three Stage Model (although not a theory) is allows me to feel comfortable utilising it with clients.

Psychodynamic theory has never really been a favourite for me, a lot of the ideas presented by Freud made me feel uncomfortable. In particular, Freud's ideas surrounding penis envy and the oedipus complex are ideas which I do not feel comfortable with. However, credit given where credit is due, I recognise that Freud served as the stepping stone for the entire psychological movement, and he had many ideas which are still being researched today, such as his ideas surrounding childhood trauma (or, as it is more commonly called today, Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)).

While I generally do not like psychoanalysis or the psychodynamic field, Freud's contemporary, Carl Jung, is of great interest to me: His ideas surrounding mysticism and archetypes fascinate me, and I can almost connect to it - especially ideas such as the collective unconscious and genetic memory. I would like to do a lot more research into Jung, but I particularly found comfort in his ideas surrounding archetypes and personality - I find that much of what he said is still relevant today, particularly surrounding the idea of the anima and animus as well as individuation.

Humanistic theories are all generally ones that I find that I like. Carl Rogers's theories are very liberal; freeing. With person-centred counselling, there is no rigid path, no specific way to follow, but rather guidelines which are to be tailored to the client - I like the idea that the right answer is with the client and not the counsellor, and that the client is the theory. His paper on the necessary and sufficient conditions for constructive personality change to occur makes sense too - I believe that the therapeutic alliance generates more positive and effective results than the theory, and that the relationship is more important than the theory ultimately, so Rogers' ideas make sense to me. I have contemplated pursuing this theory and working with it as I find that I connect with it the most.

Prior to (and during) this course, I have dealt with panic attacks relating to death and what comes after. That's why Existentialism intrigues me. This theory served as an eye-opener for me and the theory itself made me feel as though I wasn't so alone. Learning about this theory helped me in coping with my own issues, however I do not recall much beyond parts which I personally identified with, and so it is a theory which I would be keen to learn more about.


Skills and qualities of effective counsellors



I have already established some of the key skills and qualities through my other writing, but the key skill that any counsellor should have is the ability to form, develop and maintain a therapeutic alliance in a safe and supportive environment. The relationship typically accounts for more therapeutic change than the theory, and so it is my view that counsellors should know how to create and form an environment where such a relationship can be fostered, as well as how to foster that relationship. I recognise that empathy and many of the counselling skills which I have learned throughout this course are necessary for a therapeutic relationship to be facilitated, and I feel that I have more learning to do in using these skills in a cohesive manner before I would feel confident in my ability to create a therapeutic alliance.

Therapists should, both outwith and within the counselling session, seek to respect phenomenology and keep an open mind towards the views of others. A modern world calls for modern counsellors and, in the UK at least, our culture is changing constantly to become more accepting and tolerant of ideas which were previously condoned - it is therefore, in my eyes, a necessary attribute of any counsellor to keep an open mind and not dismiss anyone because of a disagreement. Counsellors should accept others for their identity and views.Counsellors are not moral guardians or mindguards, and should only challenge an individual if there is therapeutic value to it. I believe that I have made a lot of progress throughout this course in becoming more accepting of new language, new ideas and new perspectives.

Additionally, relating to mindguards, ounsellors should be able to criticise their favourite theory and recognise flaws or critiques in things which they like. Being unable to see and accept holes within a theory, model or idea that you like is hypocritical if you then go on to expect clients to accept any challenges that you provide, or to accept any change. I know that I can be stubborn at times, and may not admit that there are issues with things that I like immediately out of pride, however I often find myself reflecting and recognising the validity in the points of others. I would like to work more on being able to reflect in the here and now, and let go of my pride and stubbornness when it is detrimenting me.

Counsellors should, above all else, act in the best interests of their clients in therapeutic processes. They should demonstrate candour in being open with clients, care and benevolence towards their clients, humility in understanding themselves and their abilities, and integrity in upholding their morals, boundaries, and duties. I think that I have not always acted in the best interests of clients due to me worrying about my own progress and development, but moving on I feel that I have the capability to do so and I believe that I have been moving towards these qualities.



Summary: Strengths, Weaknessess and Areas for Development



I have developed and formed a lot of strengths throughout this course. I believe that I have become a lot more tolerant of others, even when my visceral reaction is less than willing. I have developed good non-verbal behaviours for my role as a counsellor in one-to-one sessions.

In particular, however, I have become open to change. For instance, I have found it a lot easier to speak about myself in an authentic manner, unlike my first session where I struggled to speak for even ten minutes about something close to my heart. I have also changed how I end a session: rather than blatantly interrupting clients, I acknowledge what they are saying and encourage them to continue speaking about it at the next session if they choose, but let them know that our session is drawing to a close. I have also been able to expand my emotional vocabulary, allowing me to more accurately reflect the emotions that clients are expressing.



However, I do still have many weaknesses and areas to develop in.

One weakness and area for development is my communication skills. I still continue to rehearse what I am saying and engage in the shadow side of listening rather than remaining fully present with the client. While I cannot be perfect, I can strive to work in the interests of clients and develop my skills better until I have extinguished this behaviour. I also still continue to have an external locus of evaluation when I speak, I worry about what others will think of me or how they will perceive me if I say things - I need to become more confident in owning my thoughts and expressions. I also need to fine-tune my use of a therapeutic silence, knowing when to use them and for how long before it is ineffective. I also need to work on conveying empathy, and how to do so in a way which shows the client that I am really trying to listen.

Another area for development is contingency planning, and knowing how to react in various situations. I am unsure of how to confidently address boundary violations as I have never had to deal with it previously, and I would like experience of this.

While I have used all three stages of Egan's Three Stage Model, I have never had the experience of doing this with one client, from start to finish. I feel that it is vital to know how to work with a client through this process, as I believe that it can present a whole array of experiences and opportunities that would otherwise not be experienced.

I also need to work on my theoretical knowledge. While having an understanding of counselling skills and a model can help clients, having an understanding of a particular theory would allow me to adequately prepare for and resolve many more situations, such as negative beliefs holding a client back. Being able to draw upon insights from a theory allows me to adequately and ethically resolve situations which I have never encountered before.

I should also work on the skills and qualities I identified for an effective counsellor. I recognise that I do not hold a strong grasp on the ability to form and maintain a therapeutic relationship, and I would like to work on this more before working with real clients.

Personality and Attachment Tests


Myers-Briggs Personality Test

I learned that I hold an INFP personality (meaning introversion, intuition, feeling and perceiving). INFP types are allegedly sensitive, creative, idealistic, perceptive, caring, loyal. They value inner harmony and personal growth, and focus on dreams and possibilities. I relate a lot to being sensitive and idealistic, wanting to focus on my dreams rather than taking a pragmatic approach. I think that this personality type fits me best.


Eysenck Personality Test

This test reflects the ideas of Hans Eysenck a pioneer in the field of personality research. Through research and statistical analysis he determined that personality is composed of three main elements: Extroversion, Neuroticism, and Psychoticism. My results on this test were that I am low in extroversion, high in neuroticism, and low in psychoticism.

Extroversion results were moderately low which suggests I are reclusive, quiet, unassertive, and private. I both agree and disagree with this - I am reclusive and quiet around individuals I do not know, but I become a lot more forthcoming and open with people that I am familiar with. I do not think that I am private.

Neuroticism results were high which suggests I am very worrying, insecure, emotional, and anxious. There's not much to say about this other than that I agree - I am insecure and deeply emotional, however I tend to reserve these emotions.

Psychoticism results were low which suggests I am overly kind natured, trusting, and helpful at the expense too often of my own individual development (martyr complex). I used to be quite like this, and would disregard my own needs and wants in favour of pleasing others, but I have majorly changed this. I still want to make others happy, but I am making space for myself while doing so.

Grit Scale

The Grit Scale measures the extent to which individuals are able to maintain focus and interest, and persevere in obtaining long-term goals. The maximum score on this scale is 5 (extremely gritty), and the lowest score on this scale is 1 (not at all gritty).

After completing this test, I scored a 2. This sounds accurate, as I easily lose motivation at the first setback and struggle to commit to many things long-term outside of education.



Attachment Theory Test

According to the test, I have an anxious-preoccupied attachment style.

People who anxiously attach tend to worry more about their relationships. They are said to experience an ’emotional hunger’ and are desperate for a fantasy type of love. Unlike securely attached people, people with an anxious attachment tend to be desperate to form a fantasy bond of ideal love–even when this might not be possible or reciprocated. They tend to look for a partner who can rescue them or ‘complete’ them. Unfortunately, their desperation sometimes can push away the exact person they want closeness with. When they are afraid of losing their partner, they can become clingy, possessive, paranoid, or need constant attention.

While I hold anxieties surrounding the security of the relationship, I reject the idea that the partner completes or fixes me. I think I do require affection, but I don't see it as something that needs 'completed'.
